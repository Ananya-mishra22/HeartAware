What Causes Heart Attacks?

Heart attacks occur when blood flow to a part of the heart is blocked, usually by a blood clot. Without blood flow, heart tissue begins to die. Making lifestyle changes can significantly reduce your risk of having a heart attack or experiencing a second one.

Lifestyle Changes That Make A Difference

Quit Smoking

Smoking damages blood vessels, reduces oxygen in the blood, and makes the heart work harder. Quitting smoking can rapidly reduce your heart attack risk.

  • Seek support from healthcare providers
  • Consider nicotine replacement therapies
  • Join smoking cessation programs

Heart-Healthy Eating

Your diet plays a crucial role in heart health. Focus on:

  • Fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
  • Lean proteins like fish, poultry, and legumes
  • Healthy fats from sources like olive oil, nuts, and avocados
  • Limiting sodium, added sugars, and processed foods
  • Moderating alcohol consumption

Regular Physical Activity

Exercise strengthens your heart and improves circulation. Aim for:

  • At least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week
  • Muscle-strengthening activities twice a week
  • Daily movement and less sitting time

Always consult with your healthcare provider before starting a new exercise program, especially after a heart attack.

Maintain a Healthy Weight

Excess weight, especially around the waist, increases heart attack risk by raising blood pressure, cholesterol, and diabetes risk.

  • Work with healthcare providers to determine a healthy weight goal
  • Make sustainable dietary changes
  • Incorporate regular physical activity

Manage Stress

Chronic stress can increase heart attack risk. Healthy ways to manage stress include:

  • Mindfulness and meditation practices
  • Regular physical activity
  • Adequate sleep
  • Social connections and support
  • Professional counseling when needed

Control Medical Conditions

Managing these conditions is crucial for heart health:

  • High blood pressure
  • High cholesterol
  • Diabetes
  • Sleep apnea

Take medications as prescribed and attend regular check-ups with your healthcare provider.

Creating Your Heart Health Plan

Work with your healthcare team to develop a personalized plan that addresses your specific risk factors. Regular follow-ups are essential to monitor your progress and adjust your plan as needed.

If you've had a heart attack, cardiac rehabilitation programs can provide supervised exercise, education, and support to help you recover and reduce your risk of future heart problems.

Know the Warning Signs

Being able to recognize heart attack symptoms can save lives. Common warning signs include:

  • Chest discomfort or pain that feels like pressure, squeezing, fullness, or pain
  • Pain or discomfort in one or both arms, the back, neck, jaw, or stomach
  • Shortness of breath with or without chest discomfort
  • Cold sweat, nausea, or lightheadedness
  • Women may experience less typical symptoms such as unusual fatigue, sleep disturbances, or shortness of breath without chest pain

If you experience these symptoms, call emergency services immediately. Minutes matter!
